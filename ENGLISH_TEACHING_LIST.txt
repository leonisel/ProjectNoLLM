Grammar: is the system of rules for using language correctly
Noun: is a word that names a person, place, thing, or idea
Proper Noun: names a specific person, place, or organization
Common Noun: names a general person, place, or thing
Collective Noun: names a group acting as one unit
Abstract Noun: names an idea, feeling, or quality
Concrete Noun: names something physical
Countable Noun: can be counted individually
Uncountable Noun: cannot usually be counted individually
Compound Noun: combines two or more words into one noun
Possessive Noun: shows ownership
Verb: expresses an action or state of being
Action Verb: describes something being done
Linking Verb: connects the subject to more information
Helping Verb: assists the main verb
Modal Verb: expresses possibility, ability, permission, or obligation
Transitive Verb: takes a direct object
Intransitive Verb: does not take a direct object
Regular Verb: forms the past tense with -ed
Irregular Verb: forms the past tense differently
Adjective: describes a noun
Comparative Adjective: compares two things
Superlative Adjective: compares three or more things
Predicate Adjective: follows a linking verb
Adverb: modifies a verb, adjective, or another adverb
Adverb of Time: tells when
Adverb of Place: tells where
Adverb of Manner: tells how
Adverb of Frequency: tells how often
Adverb of Degree: tells how much
Pronoun: replaces a noun
Personal Pronoun: refers to people or things
Possessive Pronoun: shows ownership
Reflexive Pronoun: refers back to the subject
Relative Pronoun: introduces a relative clause
Demonstrative Pronoun: points to something
Indefinite Pronoun: refers to non-specific people or things
Interrogative Pronoun: asks questions
Reciprocal Pronoun: expresses mutual action
Preposition: shows relationships
Compound Preposition: consists of multiple words
Conjunction: connects words or clauses
Coordinating Conjunction: joins equal elements
Subordinating Conjunction: introduces dependent clauses
Correlative Conjunction: works in matching pairs
Interjection: expresses sudden emotion
Article: introduces nouns
Definite Article: refers to a specific noun
Indefinite Article: refers to a non-specific noun
Determiner: introduces or limits a noun
Quantifier: indicates amount
Sentence: expresses a complete thought
Clause: contains a subject and predicate
Independent Clause: can stand alone
Dependent Clause: cannot stand alone
Phrase: group of words without both subject and verb
Noun Phrase: functions as a noun
Verb Phrase: functions as a verb
Adjective Phrase: functions as an adjective
Adverb Phrase: functions as an adverb
Prepositional Phrase: begins with a preposition
Subject: what the sentence is about
Simple Subject: the main noun
Complete Subject: the subject and its modifiers
Predicate: tells what the subject does
Simple Predicate: the main verb
Complete Predicate: the verb and related words
Direct Object: receives the action
Indirect Object: receives the direct object
Object Complement: renames or describes the object
Subject Complement: follows a linking verb
Appositive: renames another noun
Modifier: describes another word
Dangling Modifier: incorrectly modifies a sentence
Misplaced Modifier: placed near the wrong word
Compound Subject: has multiple subjects
Compound Predicate: has multiple verbs
Simple Sentence: one independent clause
Compound Sentence: two or more independent clauses
Complex Sentence: one independent and one dependent clause
Compound-Complex Sentence: multiple independent and dependent clauses
Declarative Sentence: makes a statement
Interrogative Sentence: asks a question
Imperative Sentence: gives a command
Exclamatory Sentence: expresses strong feeling
Present Tense: describes current actions
Past Tense: describes completed actions
Future Tense: describes upcoming actions

Subjunctive Mood: expresses wishes or hypotheticals
Conditional Mood: expresses possibilities

Bare Infinitive: base verb without "to"
